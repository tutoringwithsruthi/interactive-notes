〰 Mechanical Waves — The Basics

Imagine dropping a pebble into a still pond. Ripples spread outward in circles. But here's the thing — the water itself doesn't travel across the pond. Each bit of water just bobs up and down in place. What does travel is the pattern — the shape of the ripple moves outward and carries energy with it.

That moving pattern is a wave. A wave is energy moving through stuff (we call that stuff the medium — water, air, a rope, etc.) without the stuff itself going anywhere.

Two Types of Waves
Transverse wave — "the rope wave"
Grab a rope and flick it up and down. The wave travels along the rope, but each piece of rope moves up and down — at a right angle to where the wave is heading. Waves on a string, water surface waves, and light all work this way.

Longitudinal wave — "the Slinky wave"
Push one end of a Slinky forward and pull it back. The wave travels along the Slinky, and each coil moves back and forth in the same direction the wave travels. Sound waves work this way — air molecules bunch together and spread apart.
The Six Key Quantities

There are six numbers that describe any wave. They're all connected to each other — if you know some, you can figure out the rest.

$\lambda$Wavelength (lambda) — The length of one complete wave. Measure from one peak to the very next peak, or one valley to the next valley. Think of it as the "size" of one wave. Unit: meters (m). $f$Frequency — How many complete waves pass by you every second. If you stand still and count 5 waves going past in one second, the frequency is 5 Hz. You control this by how fast you shake the source. Unit: hertz (Hz) = waves per second. $T$Period — How long it takes for ONE complete wave to pass by you. If frequency is "how many per second," period is "how many seconds per one." They are just flips of each other: $T = 1/f$. If 4 waves pass per second ($f = 4$), each one takes $1/4 = 0.25$ seconds ($T = 0.25$ s). Unit: seconds (s). $v$Wave speed — How fast the wave pattern itself moves forward through the medium. Not how fast the rope jiggles — how fast the ripple travels. Unit: m/s. $d$Distance — How far the wave has traveled. If a sound wave goes from you to a wall, $d$ is the distance to that wall. Unit: meters (m). $t$Time — How long the wave has been traveling. Unit: seconds (s).
How They're All Connected

Here's the key insight: if each wave is 2 meters long ($\lambda = 2$ m), and 3 waves pass you every second ($f = 3$ Hz), then the wave pattern must be covering $2 \times 3 = 6$ meters every second. That's the wave speed!

$$v = f \cdot \lambda$$

In plain English: speed = frequency × wavelength. You can also rearrange this to find any piece: $f = v/\lambda$ or $\lambda = v/f$.

And since speed is just distance over time:

$$v = \frac{d}{t}$$
The big rule: Wave speed depends on the medium (what the wave travels through), NOT on how fast you shake. Sound in air is always ~343 m/s whether you whisper or shout, whether the pitch is high or low. If you shake faster (increase $f$), each wave just gets shorter ($\lambda$ goes down) so that $v$ stays the same. Try it in the explorer below!

Echoes — Sound Bouncing Like Light in a Mirror

Stand in front of a mirror and shine a flashlight at it. The light travels from you to the mirror, bounces off, and comes straight back to your eyes. An echo works exactly the same way, but with sound instead of light.

You shout toward a cliff. The sound travels to the cliff, bounces off, and travels all the way back to your ears. That return trip is the echo you hear.

The key: The sound has to go THERE and BACK. If the cliff is 100 m away, the sound travels 100 m forward + 100 m back = 200 m total. That's why we use $2d$ (two times the distance to the reflecting surface), not just $d$.
$$v = \frac{2d}{t}$$

Where $d$ is the distance to the wall/cliff, and $t$ is the total time from when you make the sound to when you hear the echo.

Example: A sound wave has a wavelength of 0.753 m. What is its frequency? What is the period?
Step 1: Sound travels at about 345 m/s in air. We know $v$ and $\lambda$, so we can find $f$: $f = v/\lambda = 345/0.753 \approx 458$ Hz.
Step 2: Period is just the flip of frequency: $T = 1/f = 1/458 \approx 0.00218$ s, which is about 2.18 milliseconds. Each wave takes just over 2 thousandths of a second to pass.

📖 Key Formulas — What They Mean

Every formula here connects a few simple ideas. This card breaks down each one so you know what each variable is, why it's there, and how to think about it.

1. Wave Speed   $v = f\lambda$
Think of it like: A conveyor belt of waves. If more waves pass you each second ($f$ is bigger) or each wave is longer ($\lambda$ is bigger), the pattern moves faster.
$v$Wave speed — how fast the wave pattern travels through the medium (m/s). Depends on the medium (e.g., sound is faster in water than air), not on how hard you shake. $f$Frequency — how many complete waves pass a fixed point each second (Hz). You control this by how fast you shake the source. $\lambda$Wavelength — crest-to-crest distance (m). If you shake faster ($f$ up) but the medium doesn't change ($v$ fixed), each wave gets squished shorter ($\lambda$ down).
2. Hooke's Law   $F = -kx$
Think of it like: A rubber band. The farther you stretch it ($x$), the harder it pulls back ($F$). The minus sign means the force always points back toward center.
$F$Restoring force — the push/pull back toward equilibrium (N). Always opposes the displacement. $k$Spring constant — stiffness (N/m). A steel spring has a huge $k$; a Slinky has a tiny one. Bigger $k$ = harder to stretch = snaps back faster. $x$Displacement — how far the object is from its rest position (m). Positive one way, negative the other.
3. Mass-Spring Period   $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$
Think of it like: A heavy kid on a trampoline vs. a light kid. The heavy kid ($m$ big) bounces slowly (big $T$). A stiff trampoline ($k$ big) bounces you back fast (small $T$). Gravity and how far you pull don't matter — only mass and stiffness.
$T$Period — time for one full bounce, out-and-back (s). $m$Mass of the oscillating object (kg). More mass = more inertia = slower oscillation. $k$Spring constant (N/m). Stiffer spring = stronger restoring force = faster oscillation. $2\pi$Shows up because one full cycle is one full circle ($2\pi$ radians) of the motion.
4. Pendulum Period   $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$
Think of it like: Same idea as the spring, but now the "stiffness" comes from gravity. A longer rope ($L$ big) means a wider arc and slower swing. Stronger gravity ($g$ big) pulls harder and speeds it up. Mass cancels out — a bowling ball and a tennis ball on the same string swing at the same rate.
5. SHM Position   $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$
Think of it like: Watching a carousel horse from the side. It looks like it bobs up and down sinusoidally. This formula describes that: $A$ is how high the horse goes, $\omega$ is how fast the carousel spins, and $\phi$ is where the horse was when you started watching.
$A$Amplitude — maximum displacement. How far the object swings from center (m). $\omega$Angular frequency — how fast the object cycles, in rad/s. Related to period: $\omega = 2\pi/T$. Bigger $\omega$ = faster oscillation. $\phi$Phase constant — where in the cycle the object starts at $t = 0$. If $\phi = 0$, it starts at maximum displacement (cosine). If $\phi = \pi/2$, it starts at equilibrium (like a sine).
6. Standing Wave Harmonics   $f_n = \dfrac{n}{2L}\sqrt{\dfrac{T}{\mu}}$
Think of it like: Only certain waves "fit" on a guitar string — like only certain numbers of half-arches fit between two walls. The first harmonic ($n = 1$) is one half-arch. The second ($n = 2$) is two. Each higher $n$ packs more wiggles in the same length and vibrates at a higher pitch.
$n$Harmonic number (1, 2, 3, …). Tells you how many half-wavelengths fit in the string. $L$Length of the string or pipe (m). $T$Tension in the string (N). Tighter = faster wave speed = higher pitch. $\mu$Linear mass density — mass per unit length (kg/m). Thicker string = heavier per meter = lower pitch.
📈 Sinusoidal Waves & Trig Review

Waves in physics are described by sine and cosine functions. These come straight from the unit circle — the same trig you already know.

The Unit Circle Connection

Imagine a point moving around a circle at constant speed. If you track its vertical position over time, you get a sine wave. Track the horizontal position and you get a cosine wave. That's all a sinusoidal wave is — circular motion "unwrapped" onto a timeline.

Key trig facts:
$\sin(0) = 0$,   $\sin(\pi/2) = 1$,   $\sin(\pi) = 0$,   $\sin(3\pi/2) = -1$
$\cos(0) = 1$,   $\cos(\pi/2) = 0$,   $\cos(\pi) = -1$,   $\cos(3\pi/2) = 0$

Cosine is just sine shifted left by $\pi/2$:   $\cos(\theta) = \sin(\theta + \pi/2)$
The General Sinusoidal Form
$$y = \underbrace{a}_{\text{amplitude}} \cdot \sin\!\Big(\underbrace{b}_{\text{frequency}} \cdot (\theta + \underbrace{c}_{\text{phase shift}})\Big) + \underbrace{d}_{\text{vertical shift}}$$
$a$Amplitude — stretches the wave vertically. $|a|$ = max height from midline. If $a$ is negative, the wave flips upside down. $b$Frequency / period control — squishes or stretches horizontally. Period $= 2\pi/|b|$. Bigger $|b|$ = more waves packed in = shorter period. $c$Phase shift — slides the wave left or right. Positive $c$ shifts left, negative shifts right. In physics this is $\phi$. $d$Vertical shift — moves the entire wave up or down. The midline sits at $y = d$ instead of $y = 0$.
Connecting to physics: In $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$, the mapping is: $a = A$ (amplitude), $b = \omega$ (angular frequency), $c = \phi/\omega$ (phase shift), $d = 0$ (oscillates around equilibrium).

🔄 Simple Harmonic Motion

Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is oscillatory motion where the restoring force is directly proportional to the displacement from equilibrium and acts in the opposite direction.

Key idea: In SHM the acceleration is always directed toward the equilibrium position. The motion repeats with a fixed period $T$ regardless of amplitude (for ideal systems).
$$F = -kx \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad a = -\omega^2 x$$

Position, velocity, and acceleration as functions of time:

$$x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$$ $$v(t) = -A\omega\sin(\omega t + \phi)$$ $$a(t) = -A\omega^2\cos(\omega t + \phi)$$
Mass-Spring System
$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} \qquad f = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$
Notice: A stiffer spring (larger $k$) oscillates faster. A heavier mass (larger $m$) oscillates slower. Neither amplitude nor gravity affects the period of a mass-spring system.
$A$Amplitude — maximum displacement from equilibrium (m) $\omega$Angular frequency — $\omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T}$ (rad/s) $T$Period — time for one complete oscillation (s) $f$Frequency — oscillations per second; $f = 1/T$ (Hz) $\phi$Phase constant — determines starting point in the cycle (rad) $k$Spring constant — stiffness of the restoring force (N/m)
A 200.0 g air track glider is attached to a spring with $k = 33.5$ N/m. What frequency will the glider oscillate at if set into vibrational motion?
$m = 0.200$ kg
$f = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{33.5}{0.200}} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{167.5} \approx \frac{12.94}{6.283} \approx 2.06$ Hz

⚡ Energy in SHM

In an ideal SHM system with no friction, total mechanical energy is conserved. Energy continuously converts between kinetic and potential forms.

$$E_{\text{total}} = \tfrac{1}{2}kA^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$$
At equilibrium ($x = 0$): all energy is kinetic, speed is maximum.
At extremes ($x = \pm A$): all energy is potential, speed is zero.

Example: A 0.5 kg mass on a spring ($k = 200$ N/m) oscillates with amplitude 0.1 m. Find the maximum speed.
Step 1: Total energy: $E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2}(200)(0.1)^2 = 1$ J
Step 2: At equilibrium all energy is kinetic: $\frac{1}{2}mv_{\max}^2 = 1$ J
Step 3: $v_{\max} = \sqrt{\frac{2(1)}{0.5}} = 2$ m/s
A mass-spring system has $k = 50$ N/m and $m = 2$ kg. If the amplitude is 0.3 m, what is the total energy and the period?
$E = \frac{1}{2}(50)(0.3)^2 = 2.25$ J
$\omega = \sqrt{k/m} = \sqrt{50/2} = 5$ rad/s
$T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi/5 \approx 1.26$ s
🕰 The Simple Pendulum

A simple pendulum approximates SHM for small angles ($\theta \lesssim 15°$). The restoring torque comes from the tangential component of gravity.

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$
Notice: The period depends only on length $L$ and gravitational acceleration $g$ — not on mass or amplitude (for small angles).

🪞 Wave Reflection

When a wave reaches a boundary it reflects back. The behaviour of the reflected pulse depends on whether the boundary is fixed or free.

Fixed end: The reflected wave is inverted (180° phase shift). The boundary point cannot move.

Free end: The reflected wave returns upright (no phase shift). The boundary point is free to oscillate.

When a wave passes from one medium to another (e.g., light string → heavy string), partial reflection and partial transmission occur. The transmitted wave always stays upright; the reflected wave inverts only if the wave enters a denser (slower) medium.

Wave Reflection — Fixed vs. Free End

Fixed end — pulse inverts on reflection

A wave pulse on a string hits a wall (fixed end). Describe what happens to the pulse.
The pulse reflects and inverts. If the incoming pulse was above the string, the reflected pulse returns below the string with the same amplitude (ideal case). It now travels in the opposite direction.
🌊 Superposition of Waves

The principle of superposition states that when two or more waves overlap, the resultant displacement at any point is the algebraic sum of the individual displacements.

$$y_{\text{net}}(x,t) = y_1(x,t) + y_2(x,t)$$
Constructive Interference — "peaks meet peaks"

When two waves arrive in phase (crest lines up with crest, trough with trough), their displacements add together. The result is a wave with a larger amplitude than either individual wave.

When does it happen? The phase difference between the waves is $\Delta\phi = 0,\;2\pi,\;4\pi,\;\ldots$  (any even multiple of $\pi$).

What you see: The peaks and troughs of both waves line up perfectly. The resultant wave is taller — its amplitude is the sum of the two individual amplitudes.

Maximum constructive interference (two identical waves):
$$A_{\text{net}} = A_1 + A_2$$ If $A_1 = A_2 = A$, the combined wave has amplitude $2A$ — double the height.
Destructive Interference — "peaks meet troughs"

When two waves arrive out of phase (one wave's crest lines up with the other's trough), their displacements subtract. The result is a wave with a smaller amplitude — or even zero.

When does it happen? The phase difference is $\Delta\phi = \pi,\;3\pi,\;5\pi,\;\ldots$  (any odd multiple of $\pi$).

What you see: Every peak in one wave is "filled in" by a trough from the other. The resultant wave is shorter — its amplitude is the difference of the two.

Complete destructive interference (two identical waves, 180° apart):
$$A_{\text{net}} = |A_1 - A_2|$$ If $A_1 = A_2$, the waves cancel entirely: $A_{\text{net}} = 0$. The medium doesn't move at all.
Side-by-Side Comparison
Constructive Destructive
Waves arrive in phase Waves arrive out of phase
Phase difference: $0,\;2\pi,\;4\pi\;\ldots$ Phase difference: $\pi,\;3\pi,\;5\pi\;\ldots$
Crest + crest, trough + trough Crest + trough cancel out
$A_{\text{net}} = A_1 + A_2$ $A_{\text{net}} = |A_1 - A_2|$
Resultant is louder / brighter / taller Resultant is quieter / dimmer / flatter
What about everything in between?

Most real situations aren't perfectly in phase or perfectly out of phase — they're somewhere in between. The resultant amplitude depends on the exact phase difference:

$$A_{\text{net}} = \sqrt{A_1^2 + A_2^2 + 2A_1 A_2 \cos(\Delta\phi)}$$

At $\Delta\phi = 0$ (in phase), $\cos(0)=1$ and you get $A_1+A_2$. At $\Delta\phi = \pi$ (out of phase), $\cos(\pi)=-1$ and you get $|A_1-A_2|$. Everything else falls in between — try the explorer below to see this for yourself.

🎸 Standing Waves

When two identical waves travel in opposite directions and superpose, they create a standing wave. Certain points (nodes) stay stationary while others (antinodes) oscillate with maximum amplitude.

$$y(x,t) = 2A\sin(kx)\cos(\omega t)$$
NodePoint of zero displacement — occurs where $\sin(kx) = 0$ AntinodePoint of maximum displacement — occurs where $|\sin(kx)| = 1$
Wavelength and harmonic number
$$\lambda_n = \frac{2L}{n}$$

The $n$th harmonic has $n$ half-wavelengths fitting inside the string length. As $n$ increases, wavelength decreases and frequency increases.

Resonant frequencies — string fixed at both ends
$$f_n = \frac{n}{2L}\sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}} = nf_1 \qquad n = 1,\,2,\,3,\,\ldots$$
Standing Wave — Harmonics
1

n = 1 — Fundamental: 1 antinode, 2 nodes

Example: A guitar string is 0.65 m long with $\mu = 0.005$ kg/m and tension $T = 100$ N. Find the fundamental frequency and the 3rd harmonic.
Step 1: Wave speed: $v = \sqrt{T/\mu} = \sqrt{100/0.005} \approx 141.4$ m/s
Step 2: Fundamental: $f_1 = v/(2L) = 141.4/(2\times0.65) \approx 108.8$ Hz
Step 3: 3rd harmonic: $f_3 = 3f_1 \approx 326.4$ Hz

🥁 Beats

When two waves with slightly different frequencies superpose, the result is a wave whose amplitude oscillates periodically — this is called beats.

$$f_{\text{beat}} = |f_1 - f_2|$$
Hearing beats: You hear a "wah-wah-wah" pulsation at the beat frequency. Musicians use beats to tune instruments — when the beat frequency reaches zero, the two sources are perfectly in tune.
Two tuning forks produce 440 Hz and 444 Hz. What beat frequency do you hear? What if one fork is adjusted to 442 Hz?
$f_{\text{beat}} = |440 - 444| = 4$ Hz — you hear 4 pulses per second.
Adjusted to 442 Hz: $f_{\text{beat}} = |440 - 442| = 2$ Hz — slower pulsation, closer to being in tune.
