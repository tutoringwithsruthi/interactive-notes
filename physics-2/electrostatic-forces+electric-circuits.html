Electrostatic Forces and Electric Circuits

⚡ What Are Electrostatic Forces?

Everything around you is made of atoms, and atoms contain tiny charged particles: protons (positive charge) and electrons (negative charge). When objects gain or lose electrons, they become electrically charged. The force between these charged objects is called the electrostatic force.

This force follows a simple rule: opposite charges attract (positive pulls toward negative) and like charges repel (positive pushes away positive, negative pushes away negative). This is the same idea behind why a balloon sticks to your hair after rubbing it, or why lightning strikes!

Think of it like magnets: the north pole of one magnet pulls toward the south pole of another, but two north poles push each other away. Electric charges behave the same way!

📜 Coulomb's Law — The Electrostatic Force Formula

The exact strength of the electrostatic force between two charged objects is given by Coulomb's Law:

$$F = \frac{k \cdot |q_1| \cdot |q_2|}{r^2}$$

Let's break down every term:

$F$
Electrostatic Force — the force between the two charges, measured in Newtons (N). A positive value means repulsion; negative means attraction.
$k$
Coulomb's Constant — a fixed value: $k = 8.99 \times 10^{9} \;\dfrac{\text{N} \cdot \text{m}^2}{\text{C}^2}$. It tells us how strong electrostatic forces are in our universe. Think of it as a "conversion factor" that connects charges and distance to force.
$q_1$
Charge of object 1 — measured in Coulombs (C). A proton has charge $+1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C.
$q_2$
Charge of object 2 — also in Coulombs. Electrons have charge $-1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C.
$r$
Distance between the centers of the two charges, measured in meters (m). The force gets weaker as distance increases (inverse-square relationship).

About the Electrostatic Constant ($k$): This constant comes from the fundamental properties of space itself: $k = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}$ where $\epsilon_0$ is the permittivity of free space. It's enormous — electrostatic forces are incredibly strong at short distances!

✍ Worked Example

Problem: Two charges, $q_1 = +3 \times 10^{-6}$ C and $q_2 = -5 \times 10^{-6}$ C, are separated by $r = 0.2$ m. Find the electrostatic force between them.

Step 1: Write Coulomb's Law: $F = \dfrac{k \cdot |q_1| \cdot |q_2|}{r^2}$
Step 2: Plug in values: $F = \dfrac{(8.99 \times 10^{9})(3 \times 10^{-6})(5 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.2)^2}$
Step 3: Numerator: $8.99 \times 10^{9} \times 15 \times 10^{-12} = 0.13485$
Step 4: Denominator: $(0.2)^2 = 0.04$
Step 5: $F = \dfrac{0.13485}{0.04} = \mathbf{3.37 \text{ N}}$
Step 6: Since the charges are opposite signs, this force is attractive (they pull toward each other).
✎ Practice: Electrostatic Forces
1. Two charges of $+2 \times 10^{-6}$ C each are 0.5 m apart. Calculate the force. Is it attractive or repulsive?
$F = \dfrac{(8.99\times10^{9})(2\times10^{-6})(2\times10^{-6})}{(0.5)^2} = \dfrac{0.03596}{0.25} = $ 0.144 N, repulsive (both positive).
2. A charge of $+4 \times 10^{-6}$ C is 0.3 m from a charge of $-6 \times 10^{-6}$ C. What is the force?
$F = \dfrac{(8.99\times10^{9})(4\times10^{-6})(6\times10^{-6})}{(0.3)^2} = \dfrac{0.21576}{0.09} = $ 2.40 N, attractive (opposite charges).
3. (Word Problem) Two small metal spheres sit 0.1 m apart. Sphere A has $+8 \times 10^{-7}$ C and Sphere B has $+8 \times 10^{-7}$ C. They push apart. Calculate the force.
$F = \dfrac{(8.99\times10^{9})(8\times10^{-7})^2}{(0.1)^2} = \dfrac{0.005754}{0.01} = $ 0.0575 N (repulsive).
4. (Word Problem) A balloon rubbed on hair gets charge $-3 \times 10^{-8}$ C. It's held 0.05 m from a wall with charge $+5 \times 10^{-8}$ C. How strong is the attractive force?
$F = \dfrac{(8.99\times10^{9})(3\times10^{-8})(5\times10^{-8})}{(0.05)^2} = \dfrac{1.349\times10^{-5}}{0.0025} = $ $5.39 \times 10^{-3}$ N (attractive).
🔀 From Electrostatic Forces to Electric Current

Electrostatic forces are the reason electric current exists. When we create a path (a wire) and push charges with a force (a battery), those charges flow. That flow of electrons is electric current.

To understand how current works in a circuit, we use Ohm's Law:

$$V = I \times R \qquad I = \frac{V}{R} \qquad R = \frac{V}{I}$$
$V$
Voltage (Volts, V) — the "push" or electrical pressure that drives charges through the circuit.
$I$
Current (Amperes, A) — the rate of flow of electric charge (how many electrons pass a point per second).
$R$
Resistance ($\Omega$, Ohms) — how much the material opposes the flow of current.
❤ Ohm's Law as Your Circulatory System

Imagine your body's circulatory system as an electric circuit:

Heart = $V$
The heart pumps blood, creating pressure (voltage). Stronger heartbeat = higher voltage = more push.
Blood Flow = $I$
The rate blood moves through vessels is like current. More pressure, more flow.
Plaque = $R$
Plaque buildup in arteries resists blood flow. More plaque = higher resistance = less flow.

🔋 How Batteries Work & Electron Flow

A battery has two terminals:

+ (Positive)
Where positive charge accumulates. Electrons are attracted here (opposite charges attract, just like opposite magnet poles!).
− (Negative)
Where excess electrons build up. Chemical reactions inside the battery push electrons out from this terminal.

A circuit is simply a path for electrons to travel from the negative terminal, through wires and components, back to the positive terminal.

Why does the negative want to reach the positive? This connects directly to chemistry! Atoms want stable, full outer electron shells (valence shells). When a battery creates a charge imbalance, the negative terminal has excess electrons — atoms there are unstable. The positive terminal has a deficit of electrons — atoms there are also unstable. Just like in ionic bonding, where sodium (Na) gives up an electron to chlorine (Cl) because of chlorine's higher electronegativity, electrons in a circuit flow from where they're in excess to where they're needed. Nature always seeks balance!

Batteries & Magnets: Just like a magnet has N and S poles, a battery has + and − terminals. The electrostatic force between excess electrons (−) and the deficit (+) is what creates the "push" (voltage) that drives current!

🔌 Resistance in the Real World

Resistance isn't just a physics concept — it's designed into every electronic device you use:

Why your laptop charger brick exists: Your laptop charges at higher wattage (65-100W), but your phone only needs 5-20W. The charging adapter contains resistors and circuitry that limit the voltage and current so it's safe for smaller devices. Without this resistance, too much current would fry your phone!

Current is the flow of electrons. Specifically, $1 \text{ A} \approx 6.24 \times 10^{18}$ electrons per second.

💧 Conductivity vs. Resistance

Conductivity is how easily a material lets charge flow through it. Resistance is the opposite. What makes something a good or bad conductor?

Good conductors (metals like copper, silver, gold) have free electrons in their outer shells that can move easily. The metallic bond lets valence electrons roam freely through the material, carrying charge.

Poor conductors / Insulators (rubber, glass, plastic) have electrons tightly bound to their atoms. There are no free charge carriers, so current can't flow.

Semiconductors (silicon, germanium) are in between — they can be made to conduct by adding impurities (doping), which is how computer chips work!

Why Salt Water Conducts but Pure Water Doesn't

Pure (deionized) water has almost no free ions — it's actually a poor conductor! But water is excellent at dissociating ionic compounds. When you dissolve salt (NaCl), water's polar molecules pull the Na$^+$ and Cl$^-$ ions apart. These free ions become charge carriers — positive ions drift one way, negative ions the other — creating a current.

Add salt and watch ions appear — the bulb lights up as conductivity increases!
🧠 Insulation: From Phone Wires to Neurons

We use materials with high resistance (poor conductors) as insulators to protect and direct electrical signals:

Phone & data cables: The copper wire inside carries the signal, but it's wrapped in plastic insulation. Without it, the signal would leak to other wires or shock you!

Myelin sheath in neurons: Your nervous system uses the same principle. Neurons are wrapped in a fatty insulating layer called the myelin sheath. It prevents signal leakage and speeds up nerve impulses. Without myelin, signals would be slow and scattered — diseases like Multiple Sclerosis damage myelin, disrupting signals.

🖦 Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

Kirchhoff's Current Law says: the total current entering a junction (node) equals the total current leaving it. No charge is created or destroyed!

$$\sum I_{\text{in}} = \sum I_{\text{out}}$$

Think of a water pipe that splits into branches. The total water flowing in must equal the total flowing out. Similarly, a large artery splitting into tiny capillaries — the total blood flow is conserved!

⚗ Series vs. Parallel Circuits

Series Circuits

Components are connected end-to-end in a single path. Current is the same through every component, but voltage divides.

$$R_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \ldots$$ $$I_{\text{total}} = I_1 = I_2 = \ldots$$ $$V_{\text{total}} = V_1 + V_2 + \ldots$$

Parallel Circuits

Components are connected across the same two points, creating multiple paths. Voltage is the same across each branch, but current divides.

$$\frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \ldots$$ $$V_{\text{total}} = V_1 = V_2 = \ldots$$ $$I_{\text{total}} = I_1 + I_2 + \ldots$$

⚡ Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law says: the sum of all voltage gains and drops around any closed loop equals zero.

$$\sum V = 0 \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad V_{\text{battery}} - V_{R_1} - V_{R_2} - \ldots = 0$$

✎ Practice: Ohm's Law & Circuits
1. A 9V battery is connected to a 3Ω resistor. What is the current?
$I = \dfrac{V}{R} = \dfrac{9}{3} = $ 3 A
2. A circuit has 2A current and 10Ω resistance. What's the voltage?
$V = IR = 2 \times 10 = $ 20 V
3. (Word Problem) A student builds a circuit with a 12V battery and two resistors of 4Ω and 8Ω in series. Find total resistance, current, and voltage across each.
$R_{\text{total}} = 4 + 8 = 12\Omega$. $I = \dfrac{12}{12} = $ 1A. $V_1 = 1\times4 = $ 4V. $V_2 = 1\times8 = $ 8V. Check: $4+8 = 12\text{V}$ ✓
4. Two 6Ω resistors in parallel. What is total resistance?
$\dfrac{1}{R} = \dfrac{1}{6} + \dfrac{1}{6} = \dfrac{2}{6} = \dfrac{1}{3}$. $R_{\text{total}} = $ 3Ω
5. (Word Problem) Your phone charger converts 120V to 5V. If the phone draws 2A, how much effective resistance does the charger add?
Charger drops $120 - 5 = 115\text{V}$. $R = \dfrac{V}{I} = \dfrac{115}{2} = $ 57.5Ω
✎ Practice: Kirchhoff's Laws
1. At a junction, 5A flows in. One branch carries 2A out. How much in the other?
KCL: $I_{\text{in}} = I_{\text{out}}$. $5 = 2 + I_2$. $I_2 = $ 3A
2. A loop has a 24V battery and three equal resistors. Voltage drop across each?
KVL: $24 - V_1 - V_2 - V_3 = 0$. Equal resistors: $V = \dfrac{24}{3} = $ 8V each
3. (Word Problem) Water enters a pipe at 10 L/s and splits into 4 equal pipes. How much in each? Relate to a circuit with 10A and 4 equal branches.
Each pipe: $\dfrac{10}{4} = $ 2.5 L/s. By KCL each branch: $\dfrac{10}{4} = $ 2.5A. Same conservation principle!
4. (Word Problem) 9V battery, three series resistors: 1Ω, 2Ω, 3Ω. Find current and verify KVL.
$R_{\text{total}} = 6\Omega$. $I = \dfrac{9}{6} = 1.5\text{A}$. Drops: $1.5(1)=1.5\text{V}$, $1.5(2)=3\text{V}$, $1.5(3)=4.5\text{V}$. Check: $1.5+3+4.5=9\text{V}$ ✓
🌟 Wrapping It All Together

Let's connect everything we've learned:

1. Electrostatic forces are the foundation — opposite charges attract, like charges repel (Coulomb's Law).

2. These forces drive current — a battery creates voltage (charge separation), pushing electrons through a circuit, just like electronegativity drives ionic bonds.

3. Ohm's Law ($V = IR$) tells us the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance — like the heart pumping blood against plaque.

4. Conductors vs. insulators — free electrons make metals conduct; dissolved ions make salt water conduct; myelin insulates neurons.

5. Kirchhoff's Current Law — current is conserved at junctions, just like water splitting in pipes.

6. Kirchhoff's Voltage Law — voltage gains equal voltage drops around any closed loop.

From a balloon sticking to your hair, to the phone charger on your desk, to the signals firing in your brain — electrostatic forces and circuits are everywhere.
