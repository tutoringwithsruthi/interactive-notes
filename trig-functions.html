Trigonometric Functions

📐 The Pythagorean Theorem

Before we get into trig, we need one big idea: the Pythagorean theorem. It says that in any right triangle (a triangle with a 90° angle), the three sides are connected by this relationship:

$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$

where $c$ is the hypotenuse (the longest side, across from the right angle) and $a$ and $b$ are the other two sides (called legs).

Think of it like: If you know any two sides of a right triangle, you can always find the third. It's the most fundamental relationship in geometry, and trig is built entirely on it.
Example: A right triangle has legs of 3 and 4. Find the hypotenuse.
Step 1: $a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \implies 3^2 + 4^2 = c^2 \implies 9 + 16 = 25$
Step 2: $c = \sqrt{25} = 5$
A right triangle has a hypotenuse of 13 and one leg of 5. What is the other leg?
$5^2 + b^2 = 13^2 \implies 25 + b^2 = 169 \implies b^2 = 144 \implies b = 12$
📐 Special Right Triangles

Two right triangles show up constantly in trig. If you memorize their side ratios, the entire unit circle falls into place.

45-45-90 Triangle

Side ratio: $1 : 1 : \sqrt{2}$

30-60-90 Triangle

Side ratio: $1 : \sqrt{3} : 2$

45-45-90: Two equal legs. If each leg is 1, the hypotenuse is $\sqrt{2}$. Both acute angles are 45°.

30-60-90: Cut an equilateral triangle in half. Short side (opposite 30°) is 1, hypotenuse is 2, long side (opposite 60°) is $\sqrt{3}$.
Why do these matter? Every angle on the unit circle (30°, 45°, 60°, and their reflections) creates one of these two triangles. The side lengths directly give you the sine and cosine values. Memorize these two triangles, and you know the entire unit circle.
Example: In a 45-45-90 triangle, the hypotenuse is 8. Find the legs.
Step 1: The ratio is $1 : 1 : \sqrt{2}$, so each leg $= \frac{\text{hyp}}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{8}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{8\sqrt{2}}{2} = 4\sqrt{2} \approx 5.66$
Example: In a 30-60-90 triangle, the hypotenuse is 10. Find both legs.
Step 1: Short leg (opposite 30°) $= \frac{\text{hyp}}{2} = \frac{10}{2} = 5$
Step 2: Long leg (opposite 60°) $= \text{short leg} \times \sqrt{3} = 5\sqrt{3} \approx 8.66$
A 45-45-90 triangle has legs of length 6. What is the hypotenuse?
$\text{hyp} = \text{leg} \times \sqrt{2} = 6\sqrt{2} \approx 8.49$
A 30-60-90 triangle has a long leg of $9\sqrt{3}$. Find the short leg and hypotenuse.
Long leg $= \text{short leg} \times \sqrt{3}$, so short leg $= \frac{9\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}} = 9$
Hypotenuse $= 2 \times \text{short leg} = 2 \times 9 = 18$
📐 Trig in Right Triangles — SOHCAHTOA

Now here's where trig begins. In a right triangle, if you pick one of the non-right angles $\theta$, the three sides get names relative to that angle:

OppOpposite — the side directly across from angle $\theta$ AdjAdjacent — the side next to angle $\theta$ (not the hypotenuse) HypHypotenuse — the longest side, across from the 90° angle

The three main trig functions are just ratios of these sides:

$$\sin\theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} \qquad \cos\theta = \frac{\text{Adjacent}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}}$$
SOH-CAH-TOA — the memory trick:
Sin = Opp / Hyp  |  Cos = Adj / Hyp  |  Tan = Opp / Adj
Using Trig to Find a Missing Side
Example: A right triangle has a hypotenuse of 10 and an angle of 35°. Find the side opposite the 35° angle.
Step 1: We know the angle and the hypotenuse, and we want the opposite side. That's SOH: $\sin(35°) = \frac{\text{opp}}{10}$
Step 2: $\text{opp} = 10 \times \sin(35°) = 10 \times 0.5736 \approx 5.74$
Using Trig to Find a Missing Angle
Example: A right triangle has an opposite side of 7 and an adjacent side of 12. Find the angle $\theta$.
Step 1: We know opposite and adjacent. That's TOA: $\tan\theta = \frac{7}{12}$
Step 2: $\theta = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{7}{12}\right) = \tan^{-1}(0.5833) \approx 30.26°$
A ladder leans against a wall making a 62° angle with the ground. The base of the ladder is 4 m from the wall. How long is the ladder?
The 4 m is the adjacent side, and we want the hypotenuse. Use CAH:
$\cos(62°) = \frac{4}{\text{hyp}} \implies \text{hyp} = \frac{4}{\cos(62°)} = \frac{4}{0.4695} \approx 8.52$ m
📐 Law of Sines & Law of Cosines

SOHCAHTOA only works for right triangles. For any triangle (including ones without a 90° angle), we use the Law of Sines and Law of Cosines.

Law of Sines

In any triangle with sides $a$, $b$, $c$ and opposite angles $A$, $B$, $C$:

$$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$$
Use it when: You know an angle and its opposite side, plus one other piece (another angle or another side). It sets up a proportion you can solve.
Example: In a triangle, $A = 40°$, $B = 70°$, and $a = 8$. Find side $b$.
Step 1: $\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} \implies \frac{8}{\sin 40°} = \frac{b}{\sin 70°}$
Step 2: $b = \frac{8 \times \sin 70°}{\sin 40°} = \frac{8 \times 0.9397}{0.6428} \approx 11.69$
Law of Cosines

Think of it as the Pythagorean theorem with a correction term for non-right triangles:

$$c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C$$
Use it when: You know two sides and the included angle (SAS), or all three sides (SSS) and want to find an angle. Notice: if $C = 90°$, then $\cos 90° = 0$, and this reduces to $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$ — the Pythagorean theorem!
Example: A triangle has sides $a = 5$, $b = 7$, and included angle $C = 50°$. Find side $c$.
Step 1: $c^2 = 5^2 + 7^2 - 2(5)(7)\cos 50° = 25 + 49 - 70(0.6428) = 74 - 45 = 29$
Step 2: $c = \sqrt{29} \approx 5.39$
A triangle has sides 8, 11, and 15. Find the angle opposite the side of length 15.
Using Law of Cosines: $15^2 = 8^2 + 11^2 - 2(8)(11)\cos C$
$225 = 64 + 121 - 176\cos C \implies 225 = 185 - 176\cos C$
$176\cos C = -40 \implies \cos C = -0.2273$
$C = \cos^{-1}(-0.2273) \approx 103.1°$
⚪ The Unit Circle

Now let's connect everything back to a circle. Draw a circle with radius 1, centered at the origin. Pick any angle $\theta$ and draw a line from the center to the edge of the circle. That line is the hypotenuse of a right triangle, and it equals 1 (because the radius is 1).

Drop a vertical line from that point down to the x-axis. You've just made a right triangle inside the circle! The horizontal leg is the x-coordinate of the point, and the vertical leg is the y-coordinate.

The connection: Since hypotenuse = 1, SOHCAHTOA gives us:

$\cos\theta = \frac{\text{adj}}{\text{hyp}} = \frac{x}{1} = x$   and   $\sin\theta = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{hyp}} = \frac{y}{1} = y$

So the x-coordinate IS the cosine, and the y-coordinate IS the sine! You're just finding the (x, y) coordinates of a point on a circle with radius 1. The special right triangles (30-60-90 and 45-45-90) give you the exact coordinates at every standard angle.

We measure angles starting from the positive x-axis and going counterclockwise. A full trip is $2\pi$ radians (360°). Half is $\pi$ (180°).

Quadrant Signs — "All Students Take Calculus"

A handy mnemonic to remember which trig values are positive in each quadrant:

Q I (A)
All functions are positive (both x and y are positive)
Q II (S)
Sine is positive (y is positive, x is negative)
Q III (T)
Tangent is positive (both x and y are negative, so y/x is positive)
Q IV (C)
Cosine is positive (x is positive, y is negative)
Practice: What are the exact coordinates of the point on the unit circle at $\theta = \dfrac{5\pi}{6}$?
At $\theta = \frac{5\pi}{6}$ (150°), we are in Quadrant II. The reference angle is $\pi - \frac{5\pi}{6} = \frac{\pi}{6}$ (30°). We know $\cos\frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ and $\sin\frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$. In Q II, cosine is negative, so the coordinates are $\left(-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\;\frac{1}{2}\right)$.
〰 The Sine Function

Sine is the y-coordinate of the point on the unit circle. As the point travels around the circle, watch what happens to the y-value: it starts at 0, rises to 1, comes back to 0, drops to −1, and returns to 0. That up-and-down pattern is the sine wave — one of the most important shapes in all of math and science.

Think of it like tracking the height of a seat on a Ferris wheel as it spins. The wheel turns at a steady speed, but the seat goes up and down in a smooth wave pattern. That's sine!

Problem: For $y = \sin(x)$, what is the value at $x = \frac{\pi}{6}$? At $x = \frac{5\pi}{4}$?

Step 1: At $x = \frac{\pi}{6}$ (30°), we recall that $\sin\frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$. This is one of the standard unit-circle values you should memorize.

Step 2: At $x = \frac{5\pi}{4}$ (225°), the point is in Quadrant III. The reference angle is $\frac{5\pi}{4} - \pi = \frac{\pi}{4}$ (45°). We know $\sin\frac{\pi}{4} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$, and in Q III sine is negative.

Answer: $\sin\frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$ and $\sin\frac{5\pi}{4} = -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$.

Practice: Evaluate $\sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right)$. In which quadrant does this angle lie?
$\frac{2\pi}{3}$ is 120°, which is in Quadrant II. The reference angle is $\pi - \frac{2\pi}{3} = \frac{\pi}{3}$ (60°). Since $\sin\frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ and sine is positive in Q II, we get $\sin\frac{2\pi}{3} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.
〰 The Cosine Function

Cosine is the x-coordinate of the point on the unit circle. While sine tracks the height, cosine tracks how far left or right the point is. As the point goes around the circle, the x-value also makes a smooth wave — it just starts at 1 instead of 0.

Back to our Ferris wheel analogy: if sine tracks the height of your seat, cosine tracks the horizontal shadow your seat casts on the ground.

Sine and cosine are the same wave, just shifted! Specifically, $\cos(\theta) = \sin\!\left(\theta + \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$. This means the cosine graph is the sine graph shifted left by $\frac{\pi}{2}$ (90°). If you slide the sine wave to the left by a quarter of a cycle, you get cosine.

Problem: Evaluate $\cos\!\left(\frac{3\pi}{4}\right)$.

Step 1: $\frac{3\pi}{4}$ is 135°, which is in Quadrant II. The reference angle is $\pi - \frac{3\pi}{4} = \frac{\pi}{4}$ (45°).

Step 2: We know $\cos\frac{\pi}{4} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$. In Q II, the x-coordinate (cosine) is negative.

Answer: $\cos\frac{3\pi}{4} = -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$.

Practice: Evaluate $\cos\!\left(\frac{7\pi}{6}\right)$. Which quadrant is this angle in?
$\frac{7\pi}{6}$ is 210°, which is in Quadrant III. The reference angle is $\frac{7\pi}{6} - \pi = \frac{\pi}{6}$ (30°). Since $\cos\frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ and cosine is negative in Q III, we get $\cos\frac{7\pi}{6} = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.
🎨 Sin/Cos Transformations & Explorer

The parent functions $y = \sin(x)$ and $y = \cos(x)$ can be stretched, flipped, shifted, and moved up or down. The general form is:

$$y = a \cdot \sin\!\bigl(b(\theta + c)\bigr) + d \qquad \text{or} \qquad y = a \cdot \cos\!\bigl(b(\theta + c)\bigr) + d$$

Each parameter — $a$, $b$, $c$, and $d$ — controls a different aspect of the wave. Let's walk through each one:

Parameter $a$ — Amplitude

$|a|$ is the new amplitude (how tall the wave is). If $a$ is negative, the wave is flipped vertically (reflected over the midline). Think of it like turning a Ferris wheel upside-down.

New amplitude $= |a|$. If $a < 0$, the graph is reflected.

Parameter $b$ — Period

$b$ controls how fast the wave cycles. A larger $|b|$ squishes the wave horizontally (more cycles in the same space), and a smaller $|b|$ stretches it out.

New period $= \dfrac{2\pi}{|b|}$.

Parameter $c$ — Phase Shift

$c$ slides the wave left or right. The phase shift is $-c$. If $c$ is positive, the wave shifts left; if $c$ is negative, it shifts right. Think of it as starting your Ferris wheel ride at a different position.

Phase shift $= -c$.

Parameter $d$ — Vertical Shift (Midline)

$d$ moves the entire wave up or down. The midline becomes $y = d$, and the range becomes $[d - |a|,\; d + |a|]$. It's like raising or lowering the center of the Ferris wheel.

Midline $= y = d$. Range $= [d - |a|,\; d + |a|]$.

Problem 1: Identify the amplitude, period, phase shift, and midline of $y = 3\sin\!\left(2\!\left(x - \frac{\pi}{4}\right)\right) + 1$.

Step 1: Rewrite in standard form: $a = 3$, $b = 2$, $c = -\frac{\pi}{4}$ (since $b(x + c) = 2(x + (-\frac{\pi}{4}))$), $d = 1$.

Step 2: Amplitude $= |a| = 3$. Period $= \frac{2\pi}{|b|} = \frac{2\pi}{2} = \pi$. Phase shift $= -c = \frac{\pi}{4}$ (right). Midline $= y = 1$.

Answer: Amplitude 3, period $\pi$, shifted right $\frac{\pi}{4}$, midline at $y=1$, range $[-2, 4]$.

Problem 2: Write the equation of a cosine function with amplitude 2, period $4\pi$, shifted left by $\frac{\pi}{3}$, and midline $y = -1$.

Step 1: Amplitude $= |a| = 2$, so $a = 2$. Period $= \frac{2\pi}{|b|} = 4\pi$, so $|b| = \frac{1}{2}$, meaning $b = \frac{1}{2}$.

Step 2: Shifted left $\frac{\pi}{3}$ means phase shift $= -c = -\frac{\pi}{3}$, so $c = \frac{\pi}{3}$. Midline $y = -1$ means $d = -1$.

Answer: $y = 2\cos\!\left(\frac{1}{2}\!\left(x + \frac{\pi}{3}\right)\right) - 1$.

Practice 1: For $y = -2\sin(3x) + 4$, find the amplitude, period, range, and midline.
$a = -2$, $b = 3$, $c = 0$, $d = 4$. Amplitude $= |{-2}| = 2$. Period $= \frac{2\pi}{3}$. Midline $= y = 4$. Range $= [4 - 2, 4 + 2] = [2, 6]$. The negative $a$ flips the wave, but the amplitude and range stay the same.
Practice 2: Write a sine equation that has amplitude 5, period $\pi$, is shifted right $\frac{\pi}{6}$, and has midline $y = 3$.
Amplitude $5 \Rightarrow a = 5$. Period $\pi = \frac{2\pi}{|b|} \Rightarrow b = 2$. Right shift $\frac{\pi}{6} \Rightarrow c = -\frac{\pi}{6}$. Midline $y = 3 \Rightarrow d = 3$. So $y = 5\sin\!\left(2\!\left(x - \frac{\pi}{6}\right)\right) + 3$.
∕ The Tangent Function

Tangent is the ratio of sine to cosine: $\tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} = \frac{y}{x}$. On the unit circle, it tells you the slope of the line from the origin to the point $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$. Slope = rise over run = y over x — that's tangent!

But here's the catch: whenever $\cos\theta = 0$ (which happens at $\theta = \frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{3\pi}{2}, \ldots$, or more generally $\frac{\pi}{2} + n\pi$), you'd be dividing by zero. The function "blows up" to positive or negative infinity, creating vertical asymptotes — invisible walls the graph approaches but never crosses.

Think of it this way: when the point on the circle is at the very top or bottom (directly above or below the origin), the line from the origin to that point is perfectly vertical. A vertical line has undefined slope — that's the asymptote.

The tangent curve — notice the vertical asymptotes

Tangent Transformations

For $y = a\tan\!\bigl(b(x+c)\bigr)+d$, the key differences from sin/cos are:

• Period $= \frac{\pi}{|b|}$ (not $\frac{2\pi}{|b|}$, since the parent period is $\pi$).

• Vertical asymptotes shift with $c$: they move to $x = -c + \frac{\pi}{2b} + \frac{n\pi}{b}$.

• $a$ controls the vertical stretch (steepness), and $d$ shifts the graph up or down.

Problem: Find the period and asymptotes of $y = 2\tan\!\left(3x - \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$.

Step 1: Rewrite: $y = 2\tan\!\left(3\!\left(x - \frac{\pi}{6}\right)\right)$. So $a = 2$, $b = 3$, $c = -\frac{\pi}{6}$, $d = 0$.

Practice: For $y = -\tan\!\left(\frac{1}{2}x\right) + 3$, find the period, asymptotes, and describe how the graph differs from the parent function.
$b = \frac{1}{2}$, so period $= \frac{\pi}{1/2} = 2\pi$. Asymptotes at $\frac{1}{2}x = \frac{\pi}{2} + n\pi$, so $x = \pi + 2n\pi$. The negative sign reflects the graph over the x-axis (it now decreases between asymptotes instead of increasing), and $d = 3$ shifts everything up 3 units.
∕ The Cotangent Function

Cotangent is the reciprocal of tangent: $\cot\theta = \frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta} = \frac{x}{y} = \frac{1}{\tan\theta}$. While tangent is y/x, cotangent is x/y. It's also the ratio of the adjacent side to the opposite side in a right triangle.

Since we're dividing by $\sin\theta$, the asymptotes happen where $\sin\theta = 0$ — that's at $\theta = 0, \pi, 2\pi, \ldots$ (every integer multiple of $\pi$). Between each pair of asymptotes, cotangent decreases from $+\infty$ to $-\infty$ — the opposite direction of tangent.

⌀ Secant & Cosecant

These are the reciprocal functions: $\sec\theta = \frac{1}{\cos\theta}$ and $\csc\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta}$. Whenever cosine or sine equals zero, the reciprocal "blows up" to infinity — creating vertical asymptotes.

Think of it this way: if $\cos\theta$ is a tiny positive number (close to zero), then $\frac{1}{\cos\theta}$ is a huge positive number. As cosine approaches zero from the positive side, secant shoots off toward $+\infty$. From the negative side, it shoots to $-\infty$.

The graphs of secant and cosecant look like a series of U-shapes opening upward and downward, hugging the peaks and valleys of the cosine and sine waves (respectively). Between the U-shapes are the asymptotes.

Practice 1: What is the domain of $y = \sec(x)$? Why are those values excluded?
Domain: all real numbers except $x = \frac{\pi}{2} + n\pi$ for any integer $n$. These are excluded because $\sec(x) = \frac{1}{\cos(x)}$, and $\cos(x) = 0$ at those exact points. Dividing by zero is undefined, so the function has vertical asymptotes there.
Practice 2: If $y = 2\csc\!\left(x - \frac{\pi}{4}\right) + 1$, find the vertical asymptotes and the range.
Asymptotes of $\csc(x)$ are at $n\pi$. After the shift, $x - \frac{\pi}{4} = n\pi$, so $x = \frac{\pi}{4} + n\pi$. The vertical stretch is $a = 2$ and vertical shift is $d = 1$. The parent range $(-\infty, -1] \cup [1, \infty)$ becomes $(-\infty, -2] \cup [2, \infty)$ after the stretch, then shifts up by 1: $(-\infty, -1] \cup [3, \infty)$.

$\sin(x)$ $2\pi$ $1$ $(-\infty, \infty)$ $[-1, 1]$ None
$\cos(x)$ $2\pi$ $1$ $(-\infty, \infty)$ $[-1, 1]$ None
$\tan(x)$ $\pi$ None $x \neq \frac{\pi}{2}+n\pi$ $(-\infty, \infty)$ $x = \frac{\pi}{2}+n\pi$
$\cot(x)$ $\pi$ None $x \neq n\pi$ $(-\infty, \infty)$ $x = n\pi$
$\sec(x)$ $2\pi$ None $x \neq \frac{\pi}{2}+n\pi$ $(-\infty,-1]\cup[1,\infty)$ $x = \frac{\pi}{2}+n\pi$
$\csc(x)$ $2\pi$ None $x \neq n\pi$ $(-\infty,-1]\cup[1,\infty)$ $x = n\pi$

General Transformation Rules

For any trig function in the form $y = a \cdot f\!\bigl(b(x + c)\bigr) + d$:

Amplitude
$|a|$ (only applies to sin and cos; the others have no amplitude)
Period
$\frac{\text{parent period}}{|b|}$ — that's $\frac{2\pi}{|b|}$ for sin, cos, sec, csc and $\frac{\pi}{|b|}$ for tan, cot
Phase Shift
$-c$ (positive $c$ shifts left, negative $c$ shifts right)
Vertical Shift
$d$ (moves the midline/center up or down)
Reflection
If $a < 0$, the graph is flipped over the midline
