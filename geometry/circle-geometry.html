Circle Geometry

◎ Circle Basics & Vocabulary

Before we do anything with circles, let's nail down every vocabulary word you'll need. Think of a circle as a perfectly round fence — every point on that fence is the exact same distance from one special point in the middle.

Circle The set of ALL points in a plane that are the same distance from a fixed point (the center). It's like tying a string to a stake and walking around it — the path you trace is the circle. Center The fixed point right in the middle. Every point on the circle is the same distance from here. We usually label it $O$. Radius The distance from the center to any point on the circle. Think of it as the length of the string in our stake analogy. Plural: radii. Diameter A straight line that passes through the center and touches the circle on both sides. It's the longest chord you can draw. The diameter is always exactly twice the radius: $d = 2r$. Chord Any straight line segment whose both endpoints sit on the circle. A diameter is just a special chord that happens to pass through the center. Secant A line that cuts through the circle and crosses it at two points. Think of a chord that keeps going forever in both directions — that's a secant. Tangent A line that just barely touches the circle at exactly one point and then keeps going. It "kisses" the circle without crossing it. Arc A curved piece of the circle between two points. Like cutting a piece of string off a hoop. There are two types: minor arc (the short way around) and major arc (the long way around). Minor Arc The shorter arc between two points — it measures less than 180°. Major Arc The longer arc between two points — it measures more than 180°. Semicircle Exactly half of the circle — an arc that measures exactly 180°. It's created when a diameter splits the circle in two. Central Angle An angle whose vertex (corner point) is at the center of the circle. Its two sides are radii. The size of this angle equals the size of the arc it "opens up to." Inscribed Angle An angle whose vertex sits ON the circle itself, and whose two sides are chords. This one is always half the arc it intercepts — we'll explore why soon.
Circle Vocabulary Diagram

Key fact: A full circle = 360°. A semicircle = 180°. No matter how big or small the circle is, this is always true.

◑ Central Angles & Arcs

Now that we know the vocabulary, let's start with the simplest angle-arc relationship. A central angle is formed when two radii meet at the center of the circle. The beautiful thing about central angles is how simple they are: the angle and its intercepted arc are always the same measure. If the central angle is 80°, the arc it opens up to is also 80°. No tricks, no formulas — just equal.

Think of cutting a pizza. The angle of your pizza slice at the very tip (the center) is the central angle, and the curved crust edge is the arc. A bigger angle means a bigger crust edge.

Central Angle = Intercepted Arc

Types of arcs by size:

Minor Arc Less than 180°. Named with two letters, like $\overset{\frown}{AB}$. Major Arc Greater than 180°. Named with three letters (so we know which way around), like $\overset{\frown}{ACB}$. Semicircle Exactly 180°. Created by a diameter.

Arc Addition Postulate

If two arcs are next to each other (adjacent), you can add their measures to get the total. It's just like adding two puzzle pieces together. If $\overset{\frown}{AB} = 70°$ and $\overset{\frown}{BC} = 110°$, then $\overset{\frown}{ABC} = 70° + 110° = 180°$.

Worked Example 1

Problem: A central angle measures 72°. Find the minor arc and the major arc.
Step 1: The minor arc equals the central angle, so minor arc = 72°.
Step 2: The entire circle is 360°. The major arc is the rest: $360° - 72° = 288°$.
Answer: Minor arc = 72°, Major arc = 288°.

Worked Example 2

Problem: Two adjacent arcs make up the full circle. One arc is 145°. Find the other.
Step 1: Together they must equal 360°.
Step 2: $360° - 145° = 215°$.
Answer: The other arc is 215°.

Practice

Q1: A central angle is 130°. What are the minor and major arcs?
Minor arc = 130°. Major arc = 360° − 130° = 230°.
Q2: Three adjacent arcs in a circle measure 95°, 120°, and $x$°. Find $x$.
$95 + 120 + x = 360$, so $x = 360 - 215 = 145°$.
⌒ Arc Length

We know that arc measure is in degrees — but what about the actual physical length of an arc? Arc length is the actual distance along the curved part of the circle — if you straightened it out, how long would that piece of string be?

Think of it like slicing a pizza. If your slice has a 90° angle, that's $\frac{90}{360} = \frac{1}{4}$ of the whole pie. So the crust (arc length) is $\frac{1}{4}$ of the total circumference.

$$\text{Arc Length} = \frac{\theta}{360} \times 2\pi r = \frac{\theta}{360} \times \pi d$$

In plain English: figure out what fraction of the circle your arc covers (that's $\frac{\theta}{360}$), then take that fraction of the full circumference ($2\pi r$). Done!

Worked Example 1

Problem: A circle has radius $r = 8$. Find the arc length for a central angle of 45°.
Step 1: Find the fraction of the circle: $\frac{45}{360} = \frac{1}{8}$.
Step 2: Find the full circumference: $2\pi(8) = 16\pi$.
Step 3: Multiply: $\frac{1}{8} \times 16\pi = 2\pi \approx 6.28$.
Answer: Arc length = $2\pi$ units.

Worked Example 2

Problem: An arc has length $5\pi$ and the circle has radius 10. Find the central angle.
Step 1: Full circumference = $2\pi(10) = 20\pi$.
Step 2: Set up the equation: $5\pi = \frac{\theta}{360} \times 20\pi$.
Step 3: Divide both sides by $20\pi$: $\frac{5\pi}{20\pi} = \frac{\theta}{360}$, so $\frac{1}{4} = \frac{\theta}{360}$.
Step 4: Multiply: $\theta = \frac{360}{4} = 90°$.
Answer: The central angle is 90°.

Practice

Q1: A circle has radius 6. Find the arc length for a central angle of 120°.
$\frac{120}{360} \times 2\pi(6) = \frac{1}{3} \times 12\pi = 4\pi \approx 12.57$ units.
Q2: An arc length is $3\pi$ on a circle with radius 9. What is the central angle?
$3\pi = \frac{\theta}{360} \times 18\pi$. So $\frac{3\pi}{18\pi} = \frac{\theta}{360}$, meaning $\frac{1}{6} = \frac{\theta}{360}$, and $\theta = 60°$.
━ Arcs & Chords

So far we've looked at angles at the center and the arcs they create. Now let's look at chords — straight lines across the circle — and how they relate to arcs. Chords and arcs are best friends — when you know something about one, you usually know something about the other.

Big Rule 1: In the same circle (or in congruent circles), congruent chords have congruent arcs, and congruent arcs have congruent chords. Think of it like this: if two guitar strings stretched across a circle are the same length, they must cover the same amount of curve.

Big Rule 2: If you draw a line from the center of the circle perpendicular to a chord, that line bisects (cuts in half) both the chord AND its arc. It's like dropping a plumb line from the center — it splits everything evenly.

Big Rule 3: When you know the chord length and the radius, the perpendicular distance from the center to the chord can be found using the Pythagorean theorem. The radius is the hypotenuse, half the chord is one leg, and the distance from center to chord is the other leg.

Chord, Perpendicular from Center & Bisection

Worked Example 1

Problem: A chord is 24 units long in a circle with radius 13. Find the distance from the center to the chord.
Step 1: The perpendicular from center bisects the chord, so each half = $\frac{24}{2} = 12$.
Step 2: Now we have a right triangle: hypotenuse = radius = 13, one leg = 12, find the other leg $d$.
Step 3: $d^2 + 12^2 = 13^2 \Rightarrow d^2 + 144 = 169 \Rightarrow d^2 = 25 \Rightarrow d = 5$.
Answer: The distance from the center to the chord is 5 units.

Worked Example 2

Problem: The distance from the center to a chord is 5, and the radius is 13. Find the chord length.
Step 1: Right triangle: hypotenuse = 13, one leg = 5, find the other leg (half-chord).
Step 2: $5^2 + h^2 = 13^2 \Rightarrow 25 + h^2 = 169 \Rightarrow h^2 = 144 \Rightarrow h = 12$.
Step 3: That's half the chord, so the full chord = $12 \times 2 = 24$.
Answer: The chord is 24 units long.

Practice

Q1: A chord is 16 units long in a circle with radius 10. Find the distance from the center to the chord.
Half-chord = 8. Using Pythagorean theorem: $d^2 + 8^2 = 10^2 \Rightarrow d^2 = 100 - 64 = 36 \Rightarrow d = 6$ units.
Q2: A radius is 17 and the perpendicular distance from center to chord is 8. Find the chord length.
$8^2 + h^2 = 17^2 \Rightarrow h^2 = 289 - 64 = 225 \Rightarrow h = 15$. Full chord = $15 \times 2 = 30$ units.
△ Inscribed Angles

Central angles have their vertex at the center, and their measure equals the arc. But what happens when we move the vertex to the edge of the circle? That gives us an inscribed angle — an angle whose vertex (corner) sits right ON the circle, and whose two sides are chords of the circle. Imagine sitting on the edge of a circular stadium and looking across at two points on the opposite side — the angle your eyes make is an inscribed angle.

THE BIG RULE: An inscribed angle is always exactly half the intercepted arc.

$$\text{Inscribed Angle} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Intercepted Arc}$$

Think of it like this: the central angle gets the "full view" of the arc because it's at the center. The inscribed angle is sitting on the edge, farther away from the action, so it only sees half as much.

Important Corollaries

Corollary 1: If two inscribed angles intercept (open up to) the same arc, they are equal. No matter where you sit on the circle, if you're looking at the same arc, you get the same angle.

Corollary 2 (Thales' Theorem): An inscribed angle that intercepts a semicircle (an arc of 180°) is always exactly 90°. If the two endpoints of the angle sit on a diameter, the angle at the third point is a right angle. Always. This is one of the most useful facts in all of geometry.

Corollary 3: If a quadrilateral is inscribed in a circle (all four vertices on the circle), then opposite angles add up to 180°. They are supplementary.

Worked Example 1

Problem: An inscribed angle measures 35°. Find the intercepted arc.
Step 1: Use the rule: inscribed angle = ½ × arc.
Step 2: So arc = 2 × inscribed angle = $2 \times 35° = 70°$.
Answer: The intercepted arc is 70°.

Worked Example 2

Problem: An arc measures 124°. Find the inscribed angle that intercepts it.
Step 1: Inscribed angle = ½ × arc = $\frac{1}{2} \times 124° = 62°$.
Answer: The inscribed angle is 62°.

Practice

Q1: An inscribed angle intercepts an arc of 200°. What is the inscribed angle?
Inscribed angle = $\frac{1}{2} \times 200° = 100°$.
Q2: Two inscribed angles intercept the same arc. One is $(3x + 5)°$ and the other is $(2x + 20)°$. Find $x$.
Since they intercept the same arc, they're equal: $3x + 5 = 2x + 20 \Rightarrow x = 15$. Each angle = $3(15) + 5 = 50°$.
⟂ Tangent Lines

We've covered lines that cross through the circle (chords and secants). Now let's look at lines that only touch it. A tangent line just barely touches the circle at exactly one point — called the point of tangency. Imagine a ball sitting on the floor: the floor is tangent to the ball, and the point where they touch is the point of tangency.

THE BIG RULE: A tangent line is always perpendicular (makes a 90° angle) to the radius drawn to the point of tangency.

This is incredibly useful because it gives you a right angle, which means you can use the Pythagorean theorem!

Two-Tangent Theorem: If you stand at a point outside a circle and draw two tangent lines to the circle, those two tangent segments are always the same length. Think of it like reaching out with both hands to touch a basketball — both arms stretch the same distance.

Tangent Line, Radius & the 90° Angle

Worked Example 1

Problem: A tangent segment from an external point to the circle is 12 units. The radius is 5 units. Find the distance from the external point to the center.
Step 1: The tangent and radius form a right angle at the point of tangency. So we have a right triangle.
Step 2: Legs = tangent (12) and radius (5). Hypotenuse = distance from external point to center.
Step 3: $d^2 = 12^2 + 5^2 = 144 + 25 = 169 \Rightarrow d = 13$.
Answer: The distance from the external point to the center is 13 units.

Worked Example 2

Problem: From an external point, two tangent segments are drawn to a circle. One tangent segment is $(3x + 2)$ and the other is $(5x - 8)$. Find $x$ and the tangent length.
Step 1: Two tangent segments from the same external point are equal.
Step 2: $3x + 2 = 5x - 8 \Rightarrow 10 = 2x \Rightarrow x = 5$.
Step 3: Tangent length = $3(5) + 2 = 17$.
Answer: $x = 5$ and each tangent segment is 17 units.

Practice

Q1: A tangent is 24 units and the distance from the external point to the center is 25 units. Find the radius.
$r^2 + 24^2 = 25^2 \Rightarrow r^2 = 625 - 576 = 49 \Rightarrow r = 7$ units.
Q2: Two tangent segments from the same point are $(4x - 1)$ and $(2x + 11)$. Find the tangent length.
$4x - 1 = 2x + 11 \Rightarrow 2x = 12 \Rightarrow x = 6$. Length = $4(6) - 1 = 23$ units.
∠ Angles Formed by Secants, Tangents & Chords

We've seen central angles (vertex at center = arc) and inscribed angles (vertex on circle = ½ arc). But what about angles formed inside or outside the circle by secants, tangents, and chords? This card covers all three cases. The rule depends entirely on where the vertex is relative to the circle.

Case 1: Vertex INSIDE the Circle

When two chords cross each other inside the circle, the angle formed equals half the sum of the two intercepted arcs. Think of it like being inside a room and looking at two arcs on the wall — you average them out.

$$\text{Angle} = \frac{1}{2}(\text{arc}_1 + \text{arc}_2)$$

Case 2: Vertex ON the Circle

This is our inscribed angle rule again! Also works for a tangent-chord angle (where one side is a tangent and the other is a chord). The angle equals half the intercepted arc.

$$\text{Angle} = \frac{1}{2}(\text{intercepted arc})$$

Case 3: Vertex OUTSIDE the Circle

When the vertex is outside — whether it's two secants, a secant and a tangent, or two tangents — the angle equals half the difference of the two intercepted arcs. You take the far (bigger) arc, subtract the near (smaller) arc, and halve the result.

$$\text{Angle} = \frac{1}{2}(\text{far arc} - \text{near arc})$$

Summary of the three angle rules:

Inside: angle = ½(sum of arcs)

On: angle = ½(intercepted arc)

Outside: angle = ½(far arc − near arc)

Memory trick: As the vertex moves from inside → on → outside the circle, the formula goes from add → just the arc → subtract.

Worked Example — Vertex Inside

Problem: Two chords intersect inside a circle. The intercepted arcs are 80° and 40°. Find the angle.
Step 1: Vertex is inside, so use: angle = ½(arc₁ + arc₂).
Step 2: Angle = $\frac{1}{2}(80 + 40) = \frac{1}{2}(120) = 60°$.
Answer: The angle is 60°.

Worked Example — Vertex On

Problem: A tangent and a chord meet at a point on the circle. The intercepted arc is 148°. Find the angle.
Step 1: Vertex is on the circle, so use: angle = ½(intercepted arc).
Step 2: Angle = $\frac{1}{2}(148) = 74°$.
Answer: The angle is 74°.

Worked Example — Vertex Outside

Problem: Two secants from an external point intercept arcs of 140° (far) and 50° (near). Find the angle.
Step 1: Vertex is outside, so use: angle = ½(far arc − near arc).
Step 2: Angle = $\frac{1}{2}(140 - 50) = \frac{1}{2}(90) = 45°$.
Answer: The angle is 45°.

Practice

Q1: Two chords intersect inside a circle. The two intercepted arcs are 110° and 70°. Find the angle.
Angle = $\frac{1}{2}(110 + 70) = \frac{1}{2}(180) = 90°$.
Q2: A tangent-chord angle intercepts an arc of 96°. Find the angle.
Vertex is on the circle: angle = $\frac{1}{2}(96) = 48°$.
Q3: Two tangents from an external point create a far arc of 240° and a near arc of 120°. Find the angle.
Vertex is outside: angle = $\frac{1}{2}(240 - 120) = \frac{1}{2}(120) = 60°$.
✕ Segment Lengths in Circles (Power of a Point)

The previous card was about angles. Now let's look at lengths. When lines pass through or touch a circle, the segments they create have a special relationship. No matter which line you draw through the same point, the "product" of the segments stays the same. This is called the Power of a Point.

Case 1: Intersecting Chords

Two chords cross inside the circle, creating four segments. The products of the pieces of each chord are equal. Think of it like a balance — the pieces always multiply to the same number.

$$a \times b = c \times d$$

Case 2: Two Secants from an External Point

Two secant lines from the same external point P. Label the first secant's full length $a$ and its external segment $b$. Label the second secant's full length $c$ and its external segment $d$. The products are equal:

$$a \times b = c \times d$$

($a$ = whole length of secant 1, $b$ = external part of secant 1, $c$ = whole length of secant 2, $d$ = external part of secant 2)

Case 3: Tangent-Secant from an External Point

One tangent and one secant from the same external point P. Let $a$ be the tangent length, $b$ be the whole secant length, and $c$ be the external part of the secant. The tangent squared equals the product of the secant parts:

$$a^2 = b \times c$$

Power of a Point — the product is always the same no matter which line you draw through the point. Whether it's chords crossing inside, secants from outside, or a tangent and secant — the products always balance out.

Worked Example — Intersecting Chords

Problem: Two chords intersect inside a circle. One chord is split into segments of 6 and 8. The other chord has one segment of 4. Find the other segment.
Step 1: Use $a \times b = c \times d$: $6 \times 8 = 4 \times d$.
Step 2: $48 = 4d \Rightarrow d = 12$.
Answer: The missing segment is 12 units.

Worked Example — Two Secants

Problem: Two secants from point P. Secant 1: $a = 15$ (whole), $b = 5$ (external). Secant 2: $c = x$ (whole), $d = 3$ (external). Find $x$.
Step 1: $a \times b = c \times d$.
Step 2: $15 \times 5 = x \times 3 \Rightarrow 75 = 3x \Rightarrow x = 25$.
Answer: The whole length of secant 2 is 25 units.

Worked Example — Tangent-Secant

Problem: From point P, a tangent $a = 8$ and a secant with whole length $b$ and external part $c = 4$. Find $b$.
Step 1: $a^2 = b \times c$.
Step 2: $8^2 = b \times 4 \Rightarrow 64 = 4b \Rightarrow b = 16$.
Answer: The whole secant is 16 units.

Practice

Q1: Two chords intersect. One is split into 3 and 10. The other has one segment of 5. Find the other segment.
$3 \times 10 = 5 \times d \Rightarrow 30 = 5d \Rightarrow d = 6$ units.
Q2: Two secants from point P: $a = 20$, $b = 4$, $d = 5$. Find $c$ (whole length of secant 2).
$a \times b = c \times d \Rightarrow 20 \times 4 = c \times 5 \Rightarrow 80 = 5c \Rightarrow c = 16$ units.
Q3: Tangent $a = 6$, secant whole $b = 9$. Find the external segment $c$.
$a^2 = b \times c \Rightarrow 6^2 = 9 \times c \Rightarrow 36 = 9c \Rightarrow c = 4$ units.
● Equations of Circles

Everything so far has been pure geometry — angles, arcs, and lengths. Now let's bring circles into the coordinate plane. The equation of a circle is really just the distance formula in disguise. Every point $(x, y)$ on the circle is exactly $r$ units from the center $(h, k)$. The distance formula says $\sqrt{(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2} = r$. Square both sides and you get the standard equation of a circle.

Standard Form

$$(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$$

Here, $(h, k)$ is the center and $r$ is the radius. Think of it like a GPS — it tells you "every point that's exactly $r$ steps from the center at $(h, k)$."

Watch the signs! In the equation it says $(x - h)$. If the center is at $(3, -2)$, you get $(x - 3)^2 + (y - (-2))^2 = (x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2$. The sign flips!

General Form

$$x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$$

This is just the standard form expanded out. It looks messier, but it's the same circle. To go from general form back to standard form, you complete the square for both $x$ and $y$.

Converting: General to Standard (Completing the Square)

Think of completing the square like building a perfect box. You take the messy expanded terms and reorganize them into neat squared binomials. Here's the process:

Step 1: Group the $x$-terms together and the $y$-terms together. Move the constant to the other side.
Step 2: For each group, take half the coefficient of the linear term, square it, and add it to BOTH sides.
Step 3: Factor each group into a perfect square binomial.
Step 4: Read off the center and radius.

Worked Example 1

Problem: Write the equation of a circle with center $(3, -2)$ and radius 5.
Step 1: Plug into the standard form: $(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$.
Step 2: $(x - 3)^2 + (y - (-2))^2 = 5^2$.
Answer: $(x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25$.

Worked Example 2

Problem: Convert $x^2 + y^2 - 6x + 4y - 12 = 0$ to standard form. Find the center and radius.
Step 1: Group and move constant: $(x^2 - 6x) + (y^2 + 4y) = 12$.
Step 2: Complete the square for $x$: half of $-6$ is $-3$, square it: $9$. Add 9 to both sides.
Step 3: Complete the square for $y$: half of $4$ is $2$, square it: $4$. Add 4 to both sides.
Step 4: $(x^2 - 6x + 9) + (y^2 + 4y + 4) = 12 + 9 + 4$.
Step 5: $(x - 3)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25$.
Answer: Center = $(3, -2)$, Radius = $\sqrt{25} = 5$.

Worked Example 3

Problem: A circle has center $(1, 4)$ and passes through the point $(4, 8)$. Find the equation.
Step 1: First find the radius using the distance formula between center and the given point.
Step 2: $r = \sqrt{(4 - 1)^2 + (8 - 4)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$.
Step 3: Plug into standard form: $(x - 1)^2 + (y - 4)^2 = 25$.
Answer: $(x - 1)^2 + (y - 4)^2 = 25$.

Practice

Q1: Write the equation of a circle with center $(-4, 7)$ and radius 3.
$(x + 4)^2 + (y - 7)^2 = 9$.
Q2: Convert $x^2 + y^2 + 8x - 2y - 8 = 0$ to standard form and find the center and radius.
$(x^2 + 8x + 16) + (y^2 - 2y + 1) = 8 + 16 + 1 = 25$. So $(x + 4)^2 + (y - 1)^2 = 25$. Center = $(-4, 1)$, radius = 5.
Q3: A circle has center $(0, -3)$ and passes through $(5, 0)$. Write its equation.
$r = \sqrt{(5-0)^2 + (0-(-3))^2} = \sqrt{25 + 9} = \sqrt{34}$. Equation: $x^2 + (y + 3)^2 = 34$.
